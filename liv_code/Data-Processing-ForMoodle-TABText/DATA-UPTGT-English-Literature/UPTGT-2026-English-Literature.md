The Elizabethan Age, often called the Shakespearean Age, is regarded as one of the most glorious periods in the history of English literature. It derives its name from the reign of **Elizabeth I**, who ruled England from 1558 to 1603. Her reign brought political stability, national pride, and cultural prosperity, creating an atmosphere highly favourable for the growth of literature. The Renaissance spirit, which emphasized revival of classical learning and faith in human potential, strongly influenced writers of this period. As a result, literature—especially drama and poetry—flourished remarkably.

The duration of the Elizabethan Age is generally fixed from 1558, the year Elizabeth I ascended the throne, to 1603, the year of her death. During this relatively short span, English literature witnessed an extraordinary outburst of creative energy, particularly in theatrical writing. Public theatres in London became popular centers of entertainment, attracting audiences from all social classes.

The greatest writer of the age was undoubtedly **William Shakespeare**, whose plays such as *Hamlet*, *Macbeth*, *King Lear*, and *Romeo and Juliet* display profound insight into human nature. Alongside him stood **Christopher Marlowe**, a pioneer of English tragic drama, famous for *Doctor Faustus* and *Tamburlaine*, whose heroic themes and powerful blank verse influenced Shakespeare. In poetry, **Edmund Spenser** earned great fame through his epic *The Faerie Queene*, which blended moral allegory with imaginative richness. Another major figure was **Ben Jonson**, known for his satirical comedies like *Volpone* and *The Alchemist*, which critiqued human follies with classical precision.

Two outstanding features characterize the writers of the Elizabethan Age. First was their deep fascination with drama and theatrical art. Playwrights produced tragedies, comedies, and historical plays of high artistic quality, transforming the English stage into a vibrant literary force. Second was the influence of Renaissance humanism, which placed human life, emotions, ambition, love, and moral conflict at the center of literature. Characters were portrayed with psychological depth and complexity rather than as simple moral types.

In sum, the Elizabethan or Shakespearean Age marks a golden epoch in English literature, distinguished by the flowering of drama, the rise of immortal literary figures, and a lasting exploration of human nature shaped by the Renaissance spirit.
